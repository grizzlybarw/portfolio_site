TaylorSustainability Hub

Taylor had years of genuine sustainability work and no place to put it where customers could find it. We built that place — and made sure it actually felt like the former, not a press release.

Role UX Lead & Product Designer
Team Stakeholders, Agency Partners, Content
Client Taylor Guitars
Domain Editorial Design · Brand
The Problem
Taylor's sustainability work was genuinely industry-leading. None of it was anywhere customers could find it.

Taylor Guitars has a long history of sustainability leadership — the Ebony Project, responsible sourcing, supply chain transparency, industry awards. But those stories lived in print magazines, internal files, and scattered pages across the site. Customers had no central place to explore them. And sustainability was increasingly the reason younger buyers were choosing Taylor over another brand.

The goal was a digital hub that showcased Taylor's initiatives authentically — built trust through third-party validation, and supported users along their purchasing journey without feeling like a marketing brochure. That last part is harder than it sounds. Users can smell greenwashing from a mile away.

I led the UX and product design from discovery through launch: stakeholder workshops, information architecture, competitive audits, user research, wireframes, prototypes, and final high-fidelity designs, in close collaboration with developers and content teams.

Process
01
Discovery & Content Mapping
Facilitated early workshops with stakeholders to map content and define information architecture. These sessions clarified three non-negotiables: build trust with credible, authentic information; keep navigation simple and scannable; and design an experience that could grow independently while staying connected to the Taylor brand. Those priorities became the criteria everything else was measured against throughout the project.
02
Competitive Analysis
Looked at brands like Patagonia, The Nature Conservancy, and Martin Guitars to understand what made sustainability storytelling actually work. The pattern was clear: the strongest examples were evergreen, authentic, and focused on people rather than products. The weakest felt like PR — vague claims, infrequent updates, no external validation. That contrast shaped both the content strategy and design priorities for the hub. I love this part of a project. There are a lot of smart teams doing great work out there.
03
User Research
Surveys revealed that users wanted two things: quick, digestible stats they could process in a few seconds, and deeper long-form stories that legitimized the work. Trust signals — press coverage, awards, third-party endorsements — were essential, not optional. Younger demographics were factoring corporate responsibility directly into their purchasing decisions. These insights drove the emphasis on media mentions, certifications, and project narratives throughout the experience.
04
Wireframing & Testing
Sketched and prototyped multiple homepage layouts and tested them with users. The feedback was consistent: videos needed to be accessible without hunting; quote blocks humanized the story in a way that straight copy alone couldn't; and trust signals needed to be prominent, not buried in footnotes. Each testing round pushed the design toward something more balanced and easier to scan.
05
Prototyping & Development
The final design evolved into a modular system backed by a complete component library. That structure was the real deliverable — not just a designed page, but a scalable framework that let the content team add new sustainability stories without needing to involve engineering every time. Staying close to developers and content teams throughout kept the final product honest about both user needs and operational reality.
Key Screens
Featured Project Module
Each sustainability project gets its own editorial treatment — rich media, project context, and direct links to featured press coverage. Credibility built in.
Quote Section
Human voices anchored the story throughout. Pull quotes from Taylor leadership gave the brand a face and kept the experience from reading like a corporate report.
Final Design
The deployed hub brought together editorial storytelling, trust signals, and a modular component system — designed to grow as Taylor's sustainability work did.
Takeaways
Keep the user top of mind, especially when the internal team isn't.

The Sustainability Hub launched as a modular, media-rich experience that could grow alongside Taylor's initiatives. Users spent more time exploring multiple stories, felt more confident in their purchases, and were more likely to share Taylor with other players. That last metric — brand advocacy — was the clearest signal that the trust-first approach had worked.

Two things stuck with me. First: keeping users central is hardest precisely when internal teams are excited about features and organizational priorities. That's when the designer's job is to be an advocate, not just an executor. Second: content-heavy experiences need content strategy locked before design starts. Imagery and video extend timelines significantly — planning for that early is a design decision, not just a project management one.
